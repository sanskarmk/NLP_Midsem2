Part-of-Speech (POS) Tagging

Assign a lexical class (noun, verb, …) to every word in a sentence. The challenge is ambiguity: the same word can take different tags depending on context, so tagging is a sequence-classification problem.

📘

Theory

What POS tagging is

The process of assigning a part-of-speech / lexical-class marker to each word in a sentence (and across a whole collection).

Input: the lead paint is unsafe
Output: the/Det lead/N paint/N is/V unsafe/Adj

Open vs closed classes

🔓
Open class (new words appear constantly) — English has 4: Nouns, Verbs, Adjectives, Adverbs.
🔒
Closed class (small, fixed) — prepositions (of, in, by), auxiliaries (may, can, will), pronouns (I, you, she), determiners. Mostly function words.

Choosing a tagset

We could use a coarse set (N, V, Adj, Adv) but the standard is the Penn Treebank tagset (45 tags) — e.g. NN noun, NNS plural noun, VB verb, VBZ 3sg-present verb, JJ adjective, DT determiner, IN preposition, RB adverb. Prepositions & subordinating conjunctions are both IN; the word "to" gets its own tag TO.

Example: The/DT grand/JJ jury/NN commented/VBD on/IN a/DT number/NN of/IN other/JJ topics/NNS ./.

Why it's hard: words have several tags

The word "back" alone:

SentenceTag of "back"
The back doorJJ (adjective)
On my backNN (noun)
Win the voters backRB (adverb)
Promised to back the billVB (verb)

POS tagging = sequence classification

Given an observed word sequence \(w_1\dots w_n\), choose the tag sequence that is most probable over all possible sequences. Out of the whole universe of tag sequences, pick the best one:

\[ \hat t_1^{\,n}=\operatorname*{arg\,max}_{t_1^{\,n}} P(t_1^{\,n}\mid w_1^{\,n}) \]

Approaches (cost & accuracy rise together): rule-based → statistical (HMM/CRF) → neural (RNN/CNN) → Transformer → LLM (covered in Topic 7).

💡

Intuition

Tagging is "decode the grammar from context." Two clues do almost all the work:

Tag–tag context — a determiner is very likely followed by a noun or adjective: DT → NN, DT → JJ.
Word–tag preference — most words have one dominant tag; "is" is almost always VBZ.
🎲
Baseline that's hard to beatJust assign each word its most frequent tag from training. This "most-frequent-tag" baseline already reaches ~90%+ — context models (HMM/Viterbi) fix the ambiguous remainder.
🧮

Numerical / Problem

Estimate tag probabilities by counting in a small tagged corpus:

the/DT Employees/NNS pass/VB an/DT exam/NN ./STOP
the/DT employees/NNS wait/VB for/IN the/DT pass/VB ./STOP
employers/NNS fire/VB employees/NNS ./STOP

Transition probability \(P(t_i\mid t_{i-1})=\dfrac{C(t_{i-1},t_i)}{C(t_{i-1})}\); emission \(P(w\mid t)=\dfrac{C(t,w)}{C(t)}\).

Problem A — Transition \(P(\text{NNS}\mid \text{DT})\)

  1. Count DT as a source tag: it appears 4 times (the, an, the, the).
  2. Of those, DT is followed by NNS twice (the→Employees, the→employees).
  3. \(P(\text{NNS}\mid\text{DT})=\dfrac{2}{4}=0.5\). (Likewise \(P(\text{VB}\mid\text{DT})=\tfrac14\), \(P(\text{NN}\mid\text{DT})=\tfrac14\).)

Problem B — Transition \(P(\text{VB}\mid \text{NNS})\)

  1. NNS occurs 4 times as a source tag.
  2. It is followed by VB three times and by STOP once.
  3. \(P(\text{VB}\mid\text{NNS})=\dfrac{3}{4}=0.75\), \(P(\text{STOP}\mid\text{NNS})=\tfrac14\).

Problem C — Emission \(P(\text{pass}\mid \text{VB})\)

  1. VB occurs 4 times (pass, wait, pass, fire).
  2. The word "pass" is tagged VB twice.
  3. \(P(\text{pass}\mid\text{VB})=\dfrac{2}{4}=0.5\).
Answers: \(P(\text{NNS}\mid\text{DT})=0.5\), \(P(\text{VB}\mid\text{NNS})=0.75\), \(P(\text{pass}\mid\text{VB})=0.5\). These two tables (transition + emission) are exactly the HMM parameters used by Viterbi in Topic 7.
⚠️
CarefulTransitions condition on the previous tag (divide by count of the source tag); emissions condition on the current tag (divide by count of that tag, not the word). Treat upper/lower case consistently when counting.
🌍

Real-life examples

Search query understanding — distinguishing nouns from actions: "play cricket rules" vs "cricket playstation game".
Voice assistants & chatbots — separate commands/actions (verbs) from targets (nouns).
Information extraction / NER — find noun phrases / entities in finance, legal, healthcare text.
Grammar & writing assistants — detect wrong verb forms or mismatched phrases.
🛡️
Safety in agentic AIUser: "Set the timer for the oven to 10 minutes." POS tagging grounds it: set = Verb (action), the timer = noun phrase (object), for the oven = prepositional phrase (context), 10 minutes = time parameter. This stops the agent from dangerously interpreting it as "turn on the oven" — it picks the Timer API, not the Oven API.
📌
One-line summaryPOS tagging = pick the most probable tag sequence for a sentence. The hard part is ambiguity ("back" = JJ/NN/RB/VB); the parameters are just counted transition \(P(t_i\mid t_{i-1})\) and emission \(P(w\mid t)\) probabilities.
