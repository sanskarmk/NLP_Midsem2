Language Models (N-gram)

A language model assigns a probability to a sentence — or predicts the next word. N-gram models do this by counting, with the Markov assumption, smoothing for unseen events, and perplexity for evaluation.

📘

Theory

What a language model computes

A language model (LM) computes either the probability of a whole sentence \(P(W)\), or the probability of the next word \(P(w_n\mid w_1,\dots,w_{n-1})\). In short, it learns to predict the probability of a sequence of words.

Chain rule → Markov assumption

The exact joint probability factorises by the chain rule:

\[ P(w_1 w_2 \dots w_n)=\prod_{k=1}^{n} P(w_k \mid w_1 w_2 \dots w_{k-1}) \]

But we can never count long histories like \(P(\text{the}\mid \text{its water is so transparent})\). The Markov assumption truncates the history to the last \(N-1\) words:

Bigram (N = 2)
\[ P(w_n\mid w_1^{\,n-1})\approx P(w_n\mid w_{n-1}) \]
N-gram orders
ModelApproximation of \(P(w_n\mid \text{history})\)
Unigram (N=1)\(P(w_n)\) — words independent
Bigram (N=2)\(P(w_n\mid w_{n-1})\)
Trigram (N=3)\(P(w_n\mid w_{n-2}\,w_{n-1})\)

N-grams fail on long-distance dependencies: "The computers which I put in the machine room … are crashing" — the verb agrees with a word far to the left.

Estimating bigrams (MLE)

\[ P(w_n\mid w_{n-1})=\frac{C(w_{n-1}\,w_n)}{C(w_{n-1})} \]

The zero problem & smoothing

Any bigram unseen in training gets probability 0, which zeroes out the whole sentence. Laplace (add-1) smoothing pretends we saw every bigram once more:

\[ P_{\text{Add-1}}(w_n\mid w_{n-1})=\frac{C(w_{n-1}w_n)+1}{C(w_{n-1})+V} \]
Add-k (gentler)
\[ P_{\text{Add-}k}(w_n\mid w_{n-1})=\frac{C(w_{n-1}w_n)+k}{C(w_{n-1})+kV} \]

Here \(V\) is the vocabulary size. Add-1 is a "blunt instrument" for N-grams, but is used to smooth other NLP models.

Backoff & interpolation

Backoff — use the trigram if you have good evidence, else fall back to bigram, else unigram.
Interpolation — always mix all orders (usually works better):
\[ \hat P(w_n\mid w_{n-2}w_{n-1})=\lambda_1 P(w_n)+\lambda_2 P(w_n\mid w_{n-1})+\lambda_3 P(w_n\mid w_{n-2}w_{n-1}),\quad \textstyle\sum_i\lambda_i=1 \]

The \(\lambda\)'s are tuned on a held-out set. Unknown words are handled with an <UNK> token (open-vocabulary task).

💡

Intuition

The Shannon game: how well can you predict the next word? "I always order pizza with cheese and ___" → mushrooms (0.1), pepperoni (0.1)… and (1e-100). A good model puts high probability on the word that actually occurs. Unigrams are terrible at this because they ignore context entirely.

🪵
Why we work in log spaceMultiplying many tiny probabilities underflows to 0. Adding logs avoids underflow and is faster. So \(\log P(W)=\sum_i \log P(w_i\mid w_{i-1})\).
A bigram model multiplies one transition probability per adjacent pair.
🧮

Numerical / Problem

Problem A — Bigram probabilities from a mini-corpus

Train a bigram model on this 3-sentence corpus (the classic example):

<s> I am Sam </s>
<s> Sam I am </s>
<s> I do not like green eggs and ham </s>

  1. Count the start word. <s> appears 3 times; it is followed by I twice and Sam once. So \(P(\text{I}\mid \langle s\rangle)=\tfrac{2}{3},\; P(\text{Sam}\mid \langle s\rangle)=\tfrac{1}{3}\).
  2. I occurs 3 times → "I am" ×2, "I do" ×1: \(P(\text{am}\mid \text{I})=\tfrac{2}{3},\; P(\text{do}\mid \text{I})=\tfrac{1}{3}\).
  3. am occurs 2 times → "am Sam", "am </s>": \(P(\text{Sam}\mid \text{am})=\tfrac12,\; P(\langle/s\rangle\mid \text{am})=\tfrac12\).
  4. Sam occurs 2 times → "Sam </s>", "Sam I": \(P(\langle/s\rangle\mid \text{Sam})=\tfrac12,\; P(\text{I}\mid \text{Sam})=\tfrac12\).
Sentence probability: \(P(\langle s\rangle\,\text{I am Sam}\,\langle/s\rangle)=\tfrac23\cdot\tfrac23\cdot\tfrac12\cdot\tfrac12=\tfrac{4}{36}=\mathbf{0.111}\).

Problem B — Laplace (add-1) smoothing

From the Berkeley Restaurant corpus: \(C(\text{i, want})=827\), \(C(\text{i})=2533\), vocabulary \(V=1446\).

  1. Unsmoothed: \(P(\text{want}\mid \text{i})=\dfrac{827}{2533}=0.327\).
  2. Add-1: \(P^{*}(\text{want}\mid \text{i})=\dfrac{827+1}{2533+1446}=\dfrac{828}{3979}\).
  3. Evaluate: \(=\mathbf{0.208}\) — the count mass is "borrowed" and spread over unseen bigrams.
Answer: smoothing drops \(P(\text{want}\mid\text{i})\) from 0.327 → 0.21, but a zero like \(P(\text{to}\mid\text{i})\) becomes \(\tfrac{0+1}{3979}=0.00025\) instead of 0.

Problem C — Perplexity

\[ PP(W)=P(w_1\dots w_N)^{-\frac1N}=\sqrt[\,N\,]{\prod_{i=1}^{N}\frac{1}{P(w_i\mid w_{i-1})}} \]

A model assigns probability \(\tfrac{1}{10}\) to each digit of a random digit string. Then

Answer: \(PP=\big((\tfrac1{10})^{N}\big)^{-1/N}=10\). Perplexity = the model's average branching factor. Lower is better: on WSJ, unigram = 962, bigram = 170, trigram = 109.
⚠️
Common slips(1) Forgetting </s> in the sentence probability. (2) Using \(+kV\) not \(+V\) when \(k\neq1\). (3) Perplexity uses the \(-\tfrac1N\) power — minimising perplexity = maximising probability.
🌍

Real-life examples

Language models pick the more fluent option among candidates:

Machine translation — \(P(\text{high winds tonight}) > P(\text{large winds tonight})\).
Spelling correction — \(P(\text{about fifteen minutes from}) > P(\text{about fifteen minuets from})\).
Speech recognition — \(P(\text{I saw a van}) \gg P(\text{eyes awe of an})\).
Autocomplete / predictive text — your phone suggesting the next word; Google's web-scale N-gram release.
