Introduction to NLP & Applications

What Natural Language Processing is, why language is so hard for machines, the levels at which we analyse it, and the application areas that make it a multi-billion-dollar field.

📘

Theory

What is NLP?

Natural Language Processing is the field that builds computers that can analyse, understand and generate human language the way humans do. It began as a branch of Artificial Intelligence and borrows from Linguistics, Psycholinguistics, Cognitive Science and Statistics. The guiding philosophy: make computers learn our language, rather than us learning theirs.

NLU (Understanding) — mapping text/speech to meaning (e.g. parsing, sentiment, intent).
NLG (Generation) — producing fluent text from meaning/data (e.g. summaries, replies, translation output).

The six levels of language analysis

A complete language understander reasons at increasing levels of abstraction. Each level has a classic "this is correct at the lower level but wrong here" example.

Higher levels depend on the levels beneath them.
Levels of language understanding — with the instructor's examples
LevelConcernsExample
MorphologicalHow words are built from morphemes (smallest meaning units)"friend" + "-ly" → friendly (noun → adjective)
LexicalListing & categorising valid wordsfriendship ✓ but friendful ✗
SyntacticHow words combine into grammatical sentences"Large have green ideas nose" — lexically ok, syntactically wrong
SemanticContext-independent meaning of words & sentences"Green ideas have large noses" — syntactically ok, semantically wrong
PragmaticHow sentences are used in a situation"She cuts banana with a pen" — semantically ok, pragmatically useless
DiscourseHow earlier sentences shape later ones"Chetana did PhD at IIT Bombay. She is a Professor at BITS." (who is "she"?)

Why is NLP hard? Ambiguity at every level

The instructor's framing: Language = Words + Rules + Exceptions, and ambiguity appears everywhere. Three exam-relevant types:

Structural ambiguity — one sentence, several parse structures: "Visiting relatives can be a nuisance." (the act of visiting vs. relatives who visit).
Lexical ambiguity — a word has multiple senses. Homonymy: bank (river) vs. bank (finance). Polysemy: related senses of "understand".
Grammatical/POS ambiguity — can = noun (container), modal verb, or verb (to can/pack).

Representations: a tiny Context-Free Grammar

Syntactic structure is often captured with CFG rules and a lexicon:

S → NP VP  |  NP → N N  |  NP → N  |  VP → V NP  |  VP → V PP  |  PP → P NP

Lexicon: Rice:N Flies:N,V Like:V,P Sand:N

Because Flies and Like belong to multiple categories, "Rice flies like sand" has more than one valid parse — a textbook case of structural ambiguity driven by lexical ambiguity.

💡

Intuition

Humans resolve ambiguity so effortlessly that language looks obvious — but we are quietly using world knowledge, context and intention. The famous HAL 9000 dialogue ("Open the pod bay doors, HAL." / "I'm sorry, Dave. I'm afraid I can't do that.") shows how much a real understander needs: knowledge of the world, the meaning of the text, and the intention behind it, plus emotional and situational awareness.

🧠
The mental model to carry into the examEvery NLP task is really "remove ambiguity using context." Tokenisation removes surface noise; POS tagging removes grammatical ambiguity; vector semantics removes lexical ambiguity; language models score which interpretation is more likely.

Why does language keep being hard? Because it keeps changing — new words, new rules, new exceptions — and the source signal (typed text, OCR, speech) is noisy.

🧮

Numerical / Problem

"Problem" questions on the introduction usually test evaluation metrics (from the contingency table) or asking you to classify the level of an error. Here is the metrics workhorse.

Evaluating an NLP system: precision, recall, F1

Given a contingency table of system decisions vs. truth:

Definitions
\[ \text{Precision}=\frac{TP}{TP+FP}\qquad \text{Recall}=\frac{TP}{TP+FN}\qquad F_1=\frac{2\,PR}{P+R} \]

Worked example. A spam filter is run on 100 emails. It flags 40 as spam; of those, 30 are truly spam. The dataset contains 45 spam emails in total.

  1. Identify the counts. Flagged spam that is truly spam: \(TP=30\). Flagged but actually ham: \(FP=40-30=10\). True spam it missed: \(FN=45-30=15\).
  2. Precision \(=\dfrac{30}{30+10}=\dfrac{30}{40}=0.75\) → 75% of flagged emails really were spam.
  3. Recall \(=\dfrac{30}{30+15}=\dfrac{30}{45}=0.667\) → it caught 66.7% of all spam.
  4. \(F_1=\dfrac{2(0.75)(0.667)}{0.75+0.667}=\dfrac{1.0}{1.417}=0.706\).
Answer: Precision = 0.75, Recall = 0.667, F₁ ≈ 0.71. The filter is fairly precise but misses about a third of spam — F₁ balances the two.
