1

Introduction & Applications

4 marks
1
Identify the level of language violated
Conceptual
Q. For each sentence, state which level of language analysis it violates: (a) "Large have green ideas nose"; (b) "Green ideas have large noses"; (c) "She cuts banana with a pen".
Show solution
  1. (a) Syntactic. The words are all valid (lexically fine) but they are not arranged into a grammatical structure — subject/verb/object order is broken.
  2. (b) Semantic. It is perfectly grammatical (syntactically fine) but meaningless — ideas cannot literally be green or have noses.
  3. (c) Pragmatic. It is grammatical and even semantically parse-able, but in real-world use it serves no sensible purpose (you don't cut a banana with a pen).
Answer: (a) syntactic, (b) semantic, (c) pragmatic. Each higher level can be violated while the lower one is satisfied.
2
Evaluate an NER system
Numerical
Q. A Named-Entity Recognition system predicts 50 entities, of which 35 are correct. The gold standard contains 40 true entities. Compute precision, recall and F₁.
Show solution
  1. \(TP=35\); \(FP=50-35=15\); \(FN=40-35=5\).
  2. Precision \(=\dfrac{35}{35+15}=\dfrac{35}{50}=0.70\).
  3. Recall \(=\dfrac{35}{35+5}=\dfrac{35}{40}=0.875\).
  4. \(F_1=\dfrac{2(0.70)(0.875)}{0.70+0.875}=\dfrac{1.225}{1.575}=0.778\).
Answer: P = 0.70, R = 0.875, F₁ ≈ 0.78.
3
Classify the ambiguity & name an application
Scenario
Q. A chatbot receives: "I saw the man with the telescope." (a) What kind of ambiguity is this? (b) Name two more ambiguity types with an example each. (c) Which NLP application most directly needs this resolved?
Show solution
  1. (a) Structural ambiguity — did I use the telescope to see, or did the man have the telescope? Two valid parse structures.
  2. (b) Lexical: "bank" = river bank vs financial bank (homonymy). Grammatical/POS: "can" = noun (container) / modal / verb.
  3. (c) Any understanding task — e.g. question answering or a dialogue/voice assistant — must commit to one interpretation to act correctly.
Answer: structural ambiguity; resolved using context/world-knowledge; critical for QA and dialogue systems.

Language Models (N-gram)

4 marks
1
Bigram probabilities & sentence probability
Numerical
Q. Train a bigram model on:
<s> I love NLP </s> · <s> I love deep learning </s> · <s> I study NLP </s>
Find \(P(\text{love}\mid\text{I})\), \(P(\text{NLP}\mid\text{love})\), and \(P(\langle s\rangle\text{ I love NLP }\langle/s\rangle)\).
Show solution
  1. \(\langle s\rangle\) occurs 3 times, always followed by I → \(P(\text{I}\mid\langle s\rangle)=3/3=1\).
  2. I occurs 3 times → "I love" ×2, "I study" ×1 → \(P(\text{love}\mid\text{I})=\dfrac{2}{3}\).
  3. love occurs 2 times → "love NLP" ×1, "love deep" ×1 → \(P(\text{NLP}\mid\text{love})=\dfrac{1}{2}\).
  4. NLP occurs 2 times, both followed by \(\langle/s\rangle\) → \(P(\langle/s\rangle\mid\text{NLP})=1\).
  5. Sentence: \(1\times\tfrac23\times\tfrac12\times1=\tfrac13\).
Answer: \(P(\text{love}\mid\text{I})=\tfrac23\), \(P(\text{NLP}\mid\text{love})=\tfrac12\), sentence probability \(=\tfrac13\approx0.333\).
2
Add-1 (Laplace) smoothing
Numerical
Q. Given \(C(\text{eat, lunch})=2\), \(C(\text{eat})=10\), vocabulary \(V=15\). Compute the Laplace-smoothed \(P(\text{lunch}\mid\text{eat})\) and the smoothed probability of the unseen bigram \(P(\text{dinner}\mid\text{eat})\).
Show solution
  1. \(P_{+1}(\text{lunch}\mid\text{eat})=\dfrac{2+1}{10+15}=\dfrac{3}{25}=0.12\).
  2. Unseen: \(C(\text{eat, dinner})=0\) → \(P_{+1}=\dfrac{0+1}{10+15}=\dfrac{1}{25}=0.04\).
Answer: 0.12 and 0.04 — the zero is now non-zero, at the cost of lowering the seen bigram from \(2/10=0.20\) to 0.12.
3
Perplexity
Numerical / Conceptual
Q. A bigram model assigns total probability \(P(W)=10^{-5}\) to a test set of \(N=5\) words. (a) Find the perplexity. (b) If a second model gives perplexity 80 on the same set, which model is better and why?
Show solution
  1. \(PP(W)=P(W)^{-1/N}=(10^{-5})^{-1/5}=10^{1}=10\).
  2. Lower perplexity = better (it assigns higher probability to the real data). \(10<80\), so the first model is better.
Answer: (a) PP = 10. (b) The first model (PP 10) is better — minimising perplexity is equivalent to maximising \(P(\text{test set})\).

Neural LM · LLM · Prompting

4 marks
1
Forward pass of a neuron
Numerical
Q. A unit has weights \(\mathbf w=[0.3,0.5]\), bias \(b=0.1\), input \(\mathbf x=[2,1]\). Compute the output under (a) sigmoid and (b) ReLU.
Show solution
  1. \(z=\mathbf w\cdot\mathbf x+b=0.3(2)+0.5(1)+0.1=0.6+0.5+0.1=1.2\).
  2. Sigmoid: \(\sigma(1.2)=\dfrac{1}{1+e^{-1.2}}=\dfrac{1}{1+0.301}=0.769\).
  3. ReLU: \(\max(0,1.2)=1.2\).
Answer: sigmoid ≈ 0.77, ReLU = 1.2.
2
Softmax over output scores
Numerical
Q. An output layer produces scores \(\mathbf z=[2,1,0]\) for three next-word candidates. Compute the softmax probabilities.
Show solution
  1. Exponentiate: \(e^2=7.389,\ e^1=2.718,\ e^0=1\).
  2. Sum \(=11.107\).
  3. Divide: \([7.389,2.718,1]/11.107\).
Answer: \([0.665,\;0.245,\;0.090]\) — the highest score keeps the highest probability and they sum to 1.
3
Why neural LMs beat N-grams; prompting modes
Conceptual
Q. (a) Using the "cat/dog" idea, explain why a neural LM generalises better than an N-gram. (b) Define zero-shot and few-shot prompting.
Show solution
  1. (a) Training saw "the cat gets fed" but never "dog gets fed". An N-gram sees "dog" and "cat" as unrelated symbols, so it can't predict "fed" after "dog". A neural LM represents words by embeddings; since "cat" and "dog" have similar vectors, it transfers what it learned about cat to dog and predicts "fed".
  2. (b) Zero-shot: give only an instruction, no examples. Few-shot (in-context learning): include a handful of solved examples in the prompt before the new input; the model infers the pattern with no weight updates.
Answer: embeddings give generalisation across similar words; zero-shot = instruction only, few-shot = instruction + examples.

Vector Semantics

4 marks
1
Cosine similarity
Numerical
Q. Compute the cosine similarity between \(\mathbf a=[1,2,2]\) and \(\mathbf b=[2,1,2]\).
Show solution
  1. Dot product: \(1(2)+2(1)+2(2)=2+2+4=8\).
  2. Lengths: \(|\mathbf a|=\sqrt{1+4+4}=3\); \(|\mathbf b|=\sqrt{4+1+4}=3\).
  3. \(\cos=\dfrac{8}{3\times3}=\dfrac{8}{9}\).
Answer: \(\cos(\mathbf a,\mathbf b)=0.889\) — quite similar (small angle).
2
TF-IDF weight
Numerical
Q. A term occurs 100 times in a document. The collection has \(N=1000\) documents and the term appears in \(\text{df}=10\) of them. Compute its TF-IDF (use \(\log_{10}\)). What is the TF-IDF of a term occurring in all 1000 documents?
Show solution
  1. \(\text{tf}=\log_{10}(100+1)=\log_{10}101=2.004\).
  2. \(\text{idf}=\log_{10}\dfrac{1000}{10}=\log_{10}100=2\).
  3. \(\text{tf-idf}=2.004\times2=4.008\).
  4. Term in all docs: \(\text{idf}=\log_{10}\dfrac{1000}{1000}=0\Rightarrow\text{tf-idf}=0\).
Answer: ≈ 4.01; a term in every document gets weight 0 (uninformative).
3
Why cosine, not raw dot product?
Conceptual
Q. Query vector \(\mathbf q=[1,1]\). Doc A \(=[1,1]\) (rare word), Doc B \(=[10,10]\) (very frequent word, same direction). Compute dot products and cosines, and explain which metric is fairer.
Show solution
  1. Dot: \(\mathbf q\cdot A=1+1=2\); \(\mathbf q\cdot B=10+10=20\). Dot product ranks B far higher just because it's longer/more frequent.
  2. Cosine: both point the same way → \(\cos(\mathbf q,A)=\cos(\mathbf q,B)=1\).
Answer: raw dot product over-favours long (frequent) vectors; cosine normalises by length so it compares direction only — the fairer similarity for words/documents.

Word Embeddings

5 marks
1
SGNS: probability & prediction error
Numerical
Q. Target \(\mathbf w=[0.2,-0.1,0.3]\). Positive context \(\mathbf c_{pos}=[0.5,0.5,-0.2]\); one negative \(\mathbf c_{neg}=[-0.4,0.1,0.6]\). Compute \(P(+\mid w,c_{pos})\) and the prediction error \((\sigma-t)\) for both words.
Show solution
  1. Positive dot: \(0.2(0.5)+(-0.1)(0.5)+0.3(-0.2)=0.10-0.05-0.06=-0.01\). \(P(+)=\sigma(-0.01)=0.4975\).
  2. Positive error: \(\sigma-t=0.4975-1=-0.5025\).
  3. Negative dot: \(0.2(-0.4)+(-0.1)(0.1)+0.3(0.6)=-0.08-0.01+0.18=0.09\). \(\sigma(0.09)=0.5225\).
  4. Negative error: \(\sigma-t=0.5225-0=0.5225\).
Answer: \(P(+\mid w,c_{pos})\approx0.498\); errors: positive \(-0.503\), negative \(+0.522\). The signs push \(\mathbf w\) toward the positive context and away from the negative.
2
Analogy by vector arithmetic
Numerical
Q. Given king \(=[0.9,0.8]\), man \(=[0.8,0.1]\), woman \(=[0.2,0.9]\). Compute king − man + woman and state which word it should be closest to (queen \(=[0.3,1.6]\)).
Show solution
  1. Dim 1: \(0.9-0.8+0.2=0.3\).
  2. Dim 2: \(0.8-0.1+0.9=1.6\).
  3. Result \(=[0.3,1.6]\), exactly the queen vector.
Answer: king − man + woman \(=[0.3,1.6]\approx\) queen — the parallelogram model of analogy.
3
CBOW vs Skip-gram & the two matrices
Conceptual
Q. (a) State two differences between CBOW and skip-gram. (b) Why does SGNS use negative sampling? (c) Which two matrices does skip-gram learn and how is the final word vector formed?
Show solution
  1. (a) Skip-gram predicts context from centre, works well on small data and rare words. CBOW predicts centre from context, trains faster and is slightly better on frequent words.
  2. (b) A full softmax over the whole vocabulary is expensive. Negative sampling replaces it with a cheap binary task: separate the true context word from a few random "negative" words.
  3. (c) A target matrix \(W\) and a context matrix \(C\); the final vector for word \(i\) is commonly \(\mathbf w_i+\mathbf c_i\).
Answer: direction of prediction + data efficiency differ; negative sampling avoids the costly full softmax; word vector = sum of target & context embeddings.

POS Tagging

4 marks
1
Count transition & emission probabilities
Numerical
Q. From the tagged corpus
dogs/NNS bark/VB ./. · the/DT dog/NN barks/VB ./. · a/DT cat/NN sleeps/VB ./.
find \(P(\text{NN}\mid\text{DT})\), \(P(\text{VB}\mid\text{NN})\) and emission \(P(\text{dog}\mid\text{NN})\).
Show solution
  1. DT occurs twice (the, a); both are followed by a noun (dog, cat): \(P(\text{NN}\mid\text{DT})=\dfrac{2}{2}=1\).
  2. NN occurs twice (dog, cat); both are followed by VB (barks, sleeps): \(P(\text{VB}\mid\text{NN})=\dfrac{2}{2}=1\).
  3. NN emits two words (dog, cat); "dog" appears once: \(P(\text{dog}\mid\text{NN})=\dfrac{1}{2}=0.5\).
Answer: \(P(\text{NN}\mid\text{DT})=1\), \(P(\text{VB}\mid\text{NN})=1\), \(P(\text{dog}\mid\text{NN})=0.5\).
2
Assign Penn Treebank tags
Scenario
Q. Tag the sentence using the Penn Treebank tagset: "The grand jury commented on a number of other topics."
Show solution
  1. Determiner "The" → DT; adjective "grand" → JJ; noun "jury" → NN.
  2. Past-tense verb "commented" → VBD; preposition "on" → IN.
  3. "a" → DT; "number" → NN; "of" → IN; "other" → JJ; plural noun "topics" → NNS; "." → .
Answer: The/DT grand/JJ jury/NN commented/VBD on/IN a/DT number/NN of/IN other/JJ topics/NNS ./.
3
Why is one word hard to tag?
Conceptual
Q. Give four sentences in which "back" takes four different tags, and explain why this makes a most-frequent-tag baseline imperfect.
Show solution
  1. "the back door" → JJ (adjective).
  2. "on my back" → NN (noun).
  3. "win the voters back" → RB (adverb).
  4. "promised to back the bill" → VB (verb).
Answer: "back" is ambiguous across four classes. A most-frequent-tag baseline always picks one tag, so it must mis-tag the other uses — only context (HMM/Viterbi, neural models) resolves them.

Statistical / ML / Neural POS

4 marks
1
Probability of a specific HMM path
Numerical
Q. Ice-cream HMM. For the observation sequence "1 3 1", compute the joint probability of the hidden path Cold-Hot-Cold using: \(P(C|\text{start})=0.2\), \(P(1|C)=0.5\), \(P(H|C)=0.4\), \(P(3|H)=0.4\), \(P(C|H)=0.3\).
Show solution
  1. Multiply start, then alternate emission and transition along the path C→H→C:
  2. \(P=\underbrace{0.2}_{P(C|\text{start})}\times\underbrace{0.5}_{P(1|C)}\times\underbrace{0.4}_{P(H|C)}\times\underbrace{0.4}_{P(3|H)}\times\underbrace{0.3}_{P(C|H)}\times\underbrace{0.5}_{P(1|C)}\).
  3. \(=0.2\times0.5\times0.4\times0.4\times0.3\times0.5\).
Answer: \(P(\text{CHC},\,1\,3\,1)=\mathbf{0.0024}\).
2
Two-step Viterbi decode
Numerical
Q. Tags {N, V}. Initial: \(P(N|\!\langle s\rangle)=0.6,\ P(V|\!\langle s\rangle)=0.4\). Transitions: \(P(N|N)=0.3,P(V|N)=0.7,P(N|V)=0.5,P(V|V)=0.5\). Emissions: \(P(\text{fish}|N)=0.6,P(\text{fish}|V)=0.2,P(\text{sleep}|N)=0.1,P(\text{sleep}|V)=0.5\). Decode "fish sleep".
Show solution
  1. Col 1 (fish): \(v(N)=0.6\times0.6=0.36\); \(v(V)=0.4\times0.2=0.08\).
  2. Col 2 (sleep), state N: \(\max(0.36\times0.3,\ 0.08\times0.5)\times0.1=\max(0.108,0.04)\times0.1=0.108\times0.1=0.0108\) (from N).
  3. Col 2, state V: \(\max(0.36\times0.7,\ 0.08\times0.5)\times0.5=\max(0.252,0.04)\times0.5=0.252\times0.5=0.126\) (from N).
  4. Best final = V (0.126); backpointer → N.
Answer: best tag sequence = fish/N sleep/V with probability 0.126.
3
HMM vs MEMM/CRF; greedy vs Viterbi
Conceptual
Q. (a) What is the key difference between an HMM and an MEMM/CRF (generative vs discriminative)? (b) Why is Viterbi decoding preferred over greedy left-to-right decoding?
Show solution
  1. (a) An HMM is generative: it models the likelihood \(P(\text{words}\mid\text{tags})\) and a prior \(P(\text{tags})\). An MEMM/CRF is discriminative: it models the posterior \(P(\text{tags}\mid\text{words})\) directly, which lets you add rich, overlapping features (suffixes, word shape, neighbours).
  2. (b) Greedy decoding fixes each tag before seeing later words, so a confident early mistake can't be corrected. Viterbi keeps the best path into every state and optimises the whole sentence jointly, recovering the globally best sequence.
Answer: generative (HMM) models \(P(w\mid t)\); discriminative (MEMM/CRF) models \(P(t\mid w)\) with features. Viterbi is globally optimal; greedy is locally optimal and error-prone.
