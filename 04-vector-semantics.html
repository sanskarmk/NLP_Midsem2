Vector Semantics

Represent a word's meaning as a vector built from the contexts it appears in. Similar words sit near each other; cosine measures that nearness; TF-IDF down-weights uninformative words.

📘

Theory

The distributional hypothesis

"You shall know a word by the company it keeps." Words that occur in similar contexts tend to have similar meanings (Harris, 1954). Two words with similar neighbouring-word distributions have similar meaning — so we can measure similarity of words by similarity of their contexts.

Lexical relations to know

Synonymy — same sense, substitutable (couch/sofa). In practice only approximate.
Similarity — share meaning but not synonyms (cat/dog, car/bicycle).
Relatedness (association) — related without being similar (coffee/cup).
Antonymy — opposite on one feature (hot/cold, up/down).
Connotation — affective meaning along valence, arousal, dominance.

Words and vectors

Build a co-occurrence matrix. A term–document matrix represents each document (column) by word counts; a term–term (word–word) matrix represents each word by counts of words around it. Two words are similar if their context vectors are similar.

Similarity: dot product → cosine

Dot product
\[ \mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b=\sum_{i=1}^{N} a_i b_i \]
Vector length
\[ |\mathbf a|=\sqrt{\textstyle\sum_i a_i^2} \]
Cosine similarity
\[ \cos(\mathbf a,\mathbf b)=\frac{\mathbf a\cdot\mathbf b}{|\mathbf a|\,|\mathbf b|} \]

Raw dot product favours long (frequent) vectors, so frequent words like the look "similar" to everything. Cosine normalises by length, measuring only the angle. For non-negative count vectors cosine ranges 0–1 (0 = orthogonal/unrelated, 1 = same direction).

TF-IDF — balancing two pressures

Frequency is useful (sugar near apricot is informative) but very frequent words (the, it) are not. TF-IDF resolves the paradox:

\[ \text{tf}_{t,d}=\log_{10}\big(\text{count}(t,d)+1\big) \] \[ \text{idf}_t=\log_{10}\frac{N}{\text{df}_t}\qquad(\text{$N$ = #docs, $\text{df}_t$ = #docs containing $t$}) \] \[ \boxed{\;w_{t,d}=\text{tf}_{t,d}\times\text{idf}_t\;} \]

A word in every document has \(\text{idf}=\log_{10}1=0\), so its weight vanishes. (A related measure is PPMI, positive pointwise mutual information.)

Sparse vs dense

TF-IDF/PMI vectors are long (\(|V|=20\text{k–}50\text{k}\)) and sparse. The alternative — learned dense vectors (length 50–1000) — generalise better and capture synonymy, leading to word2vec/GloVe (next topic).

Intuition

What does the unfamiliar word "ongchoi" mean? You see: "Ongchoi is delicious sautéed with garlic", "Ongchoi is superb over rice", "Ongchoi leaves with salty sauces". You've seen the same contexts around spinach, chard, collard greens. Conclusion: ongchoi is a leafy green — learned purely from shared context.

Cosine cares about angle, not length: digital & information point almost the same way; cherry points elsewhere.

  2. Lengths: \(|\text{digital}|=\sqrt{5^2+1683^2+1670^2}=\sqrt{5{,}621{,}414}=2371.0\); \(|\text{information}|=\sqrt{5^2+3982^2+3325^2}=\sqrt{26{,}911{,}974}=5187.7\).
  3. Cosine: \(\dfrac{12{,}254{,}481}{2371.0\times5187.7}=\dfrac{12{,}254{,}481}{12{,}299{,}260}\).
Answer: \(\cos(\text{digital},\text{information})\approx \mathbf{0.996}\) — highly similar. By contrast \(\cos(\text{cherry},\text{information})\approx \mathbf{0.018}\) — essentially unrelated.

Problem B — TF-IDF weight

The word wit occurs 20 times in a play; the collection has \(N=37\) documents and wit appears in \(\text{df}=34\) of them.

  1. \(\text{tf}=\log_{10}(20+1)=\log_{10}21=1.322\).
  2. \(\text{idf}=\log_{10}\dfrac{37}{34}=\log_{10}(1.088)=0.037\).
  3. \(\text{tf-idf}=1.322\times0.037\).
Answer: \(\text{tf-idf}(\text{wit})\approx \mathbf{0.049}\). A word like good that appears in every document gets \(\text{idf}=\log_{10}1=0\), so \(\text{tf-idf}=0\).
⚠️
Watch outUse \(\log_{10}\) (base 10) as in the lecture, square every component for the length (including small ones), and remember idf uses document frequency, not total collection frequency.
🌍

Real-life examples

Question answering — "How tall is Mt. Everest?" matches "The official height…" because tall ≈ height in vector space.
Information retrieval / search — documents and queries are TF-IDF vectors; rank by cosine to the query.
Plagiarism detection — high cosine between document vectors flags copied passages.
Paraphrase & summarisation — judging how close two sentences are in meaning.
